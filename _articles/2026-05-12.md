## The decisions

### Skip generation when yesterday was bot-only activity — *tooling-for-iteration*

Rather than let the pipeline produce a structurally empty article on days where no human commits occurred, the generator now detects the bot-only condition and exits cleanly. This prevents the compounding pattern where each low-activity day generates another low-activity article that itself becomes part of the 'previous 5 articles' context window, degrading the quality of future generation prompts. One known failure mode deserves explicit naming: if a new bot is added to the fleet and its author identity doesn't match the current detection logic, that bot's days will be treated as human days and a degenerate article will be generated silently. The guard is not self-updating — expanding the fleet means updating the author list.

### Bot-only detection as a pipeline quality gate, not a content decision — *model-behavior-as-design*

The skip logic lives in the generate step — it is a pipeline gate, not an editorial judgment. This matters because it preserves the ability to write a genuine rest-day article (which has real value: structured audit, PR triage, roadmap pulse) while filtering out runs that would produce only boilerplate. The distinction is precise and worth preserving: a rest day with zero commits is a signal worth documenting; a day where only the bot committed is a degenerate input that produces no useful output. These two cases look similar from the outside but have opposite documentation value. Locating the gate at generation time, rather than at publication time or in the content model, keeps the editorial layer clean and makes the rule inspectable in one place.
